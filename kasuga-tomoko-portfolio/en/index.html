About

Kasuga Tomoko — Chinkin Artist

Kasuga Tomoko (b. March 1978, Hachioji, Tokyo) is a Japanese chinkin artist — a practitioner of the traditional craft of carving designs into hardened lacquer and inlaying the incised lines with gold, silver, or platinum powder.

She trained at the Ishikawa Prefectural Institute of Wajima Lacquer Arts in Wajima, the historic center of Japanese lacquerware, where she studied under Mae Fumio — designated a Living National Treasure (holder of the Important Intangible Cultural Property "Chinkin") in 1999, and himself the successor of Mae Taiho, the master who first raised chinkin to the level of fine art. After a career demonstrating her craft at department stores in Tokyo, she now works primarily on commissioned pieces. She held her first solo exhibition in 2008.

「完成した漆に直接ノミを入れる。やり直しの利かないその緊張感に、心が躍った。」

"You drive the chisel straight into finished lacquer. There is no undoing a single stroke — and that tension is what made my heart leap." — Kasuga Tomoko

Her motifs center on animals and plants, carved with a prayer that each work should live — for ten years, a hundred years — carrying its maker's life within it.

The Craft

What is Chinkin?

Chinkin (沈金, literally "sunken gold") is a decorative technique of Japanese lacquer art. The artist carves a design into the hardened surface of lacquerware with specialized chisels, rubs raw lacquer into the incisions as an adhesive, and inlays gold leaf or metallic powder into the grooves. Unlike maki-e (painting designs in lacquer and sprinkling gold on top) or raden (mother-of-pearl inlay), chinkin is carved — the gold sits slightly sunken in the surface, giving a restrained, inward glow rather than outward brilliance. Because a slip of the chisel cannot be undone, every stroke is final.

The technique originated in Song-dynasty China and reached its height in Wajima, Japan, where the exceptionally durable local lacquerware — built up in as many as 124 steps — provides a lacquer surface thick enough to carve. Wajima-nuri lacquerware was designated an Important Intangible Cultural Property of Japan in 1977.

Selected Works

Works

A MAN

A portrait of Keith Richards of The Rolling Stones, in lacquer and platinum powder. Three years of preparatory drawing, one year of carving. Where convention renders human figures in negative, Kasuga carved in positive — so that the work would live.

The Heart Sutra

The complete Heart Sutra carved into a large vermilion-lacquered Wajima-nuri platter — hundreds of characters, none of which allowed a single mistake.

Onore (Self)

A horned owl whose feathers, inlaid in silver powder, slowly change tone over the decades — a work designed to keep living as it ages.
